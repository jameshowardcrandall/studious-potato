REDM Pitch Guide

How to Introduce and Position the Rapid Engagement Delivery Model

Created by James Crandall

Understanding Your Audience

Every successful pitch to a new stakeholder requires meeting them where they are. REDM is a delivery framework, but its value proposition changes depending on who is listening. This section maps the four primary personas you will encounter and explains what each one cares about, what objections they typically raise, and how REDM directly addresses their concerns. Understand your audience first, and your pitch becomes a conversation instead of a presentation.

Mission Owner / Business Sponsor

Mission owners care about one thing: operational capability. They do not care how you deliver it, what methodology you use, or how many ceremonies are in your cadence. They care that the solution works, that it solves the real problem, and that it gets into the hands of their operators as fast as possible.

What they typically say: 'We need this system in production by [date]. Can you guarantee that?' The implicit concern is schedule risk. They have been burned by projects that promised delivery and missed. They are skeptical of methodology talk because methodology is abstract; results are concrete.

How REDM addresses it: REDM commits to Initial Operational Capability (IOC) windows measured in weeks, not months. More importantly, REDM produces a working, usable system at the end of the first phase—not a requirements document or a proof of concept, but actual capability that operators can use. This means feedback comes early, course corrections happen fast, and the mission owner gets confidence in the timeline because they see progress, not promises. The language is: 'We will have operators using this system in [weeks], and every two weeks after that, you will see new capability added based on their feedback.'

Program Manager / PM

Program managers are accountable for schedule, budget, and scope. They use these three levers to manage risk. Traditional waterfall gives them a way to lock scope early (avoiding scope creep) and plan a fixed budget against a fixed timeline. The tradeoff is that if requirements were wrong—which they usually are—the entire timeline and budget have to be renegotiated.

What they typically ask: 'How will this keep us from running over budget? Won't continuous changes blow up the schedule?' They worry that allowing requirements to emerge during the build is a recipe for unlimited scope expansion and cost overruns.

How REDM addresses it: REDM fixes the timeline and the team capacity, but allows scope to vary. The PM commits to a cadence (e.g., a two-week sprint cycle) and a team size (e.g., four developers and a product owner). The scope—what features get built—is determined by the product owner in conversation with stakeholders, week by week. This means the PM always knows exactly what he is spending (the team), and he has a mechanism to manage scope: deprioritize less-important features if more important ones emerge. The result is no budget surprises and no schedule overruns. In fact, REDM typically delivers working capability faster and cheaper than traditional waterfall because feedback comes sooner, preventing large rework cycles. The language is: 'We commit to this team and this cadence. Within those constraints, we will deliver the highest-priority capabilities first, so you see the most value as early as possible.'

Contracting Officer / Acquisition Lead

Contracting officers are responsible for ensuring that contracts are structured to minimize government risk and ensure compliance with FAR (Federal Acquisition Regulation). They are used to fixed-price contracts with well-defined scopes and deliverables. Anything that sounds 'unclear' or 'flexible' makes them uncomfortable because flexibility often leads to disputes and protests.

What they typically say: 'How do I write a contract around this? What are the deliverables? How will I know when it is complete?' They need clear gates, measurable outcomes, and a structure that is defensible if a competitor protests.

How REDM addresses it: REDM structures the engagement in phases with clear exit criteria. Phase 1 is Mission Alignment (1–3 days), which produces an approved Mission Requirements Brief and a technical roadmap. Phase 2 is Rapid Build (4–12 weeks), which delivers IOC and a decision log (the record of every feature considered and why it was prioritized). The contract can be structured as a Phase 1 engagement with an option for Phase 2, or as a fixed-price delivery of IOC with a clear definition of what IOC means. The key is that REDM forces clarity early: you cannot start building until you know what 'done' looks like (for Phase 1) and what the mission priority order is. This gives the CO the structure she needs to write a defensible contract. The language is: 'Phase 1 is a fixed-price engagement to define requirements and risk. Phase 2 is a fixed-price engagement to deliver Initial Operational Capability by [date], with the scope of IOC defined in the Phase 1 output.'

Technical Leadership / CIO Staff

Technical leaders care about architecture, security, and maintainability. They worry that 'moving fast' means cutting corners on security hardening, documentation, and technical debt. They have seen too many projects that produce working code in the short term but then become unmaintainable nightmares.

What they typically raise: 'How can you deliver in weeks and still build a production-ready system? Aren't you skipping the security analysis and architecture review?' They are skeptical that speed and quality can coexist.

How REDM addresses it: REDM builds governance, security, and architecture into the build cadence from day one. It is not a question of 'moving fast now, hardening later.' The product owner's role is to weigh new features against security, compliance, and technical debt. Every sprint prioritizes some blend of features, security improvements, and architecture work. By the time IOC is delivered, the system is production-ready because security and governance were never treated as an afterthought. Additionally, REDM produces a decision log and a working system with clear patterns and documentation embedded in the code, not in separate documents that go stale. This is better for long-term maintainability than thick requirements documents that nobody updates. The language is: 'REDM is not about moving fast and ignoring quality. It is about making decisions together, continuously, as we build. Security and governance are part of every sprint, not a phase that happens after feature development is complete.'

The Core Pitch — The 2-Minute Version

If you have 120 seconds to explain REDM to someone who has never heard of it, here is the pitch. Read it out loud to yourself a few times. Make it your own. The goal is to sound natural, not scripted.

"REDM is a delivery model for building Power Platform solutions inside government. Here is the problem it solves: most government projects spend months gathering requirements, months building, and then months testing—by which time, the mission has changed, the requirements are wrong, and the whole thing has to be reworked. REDM flips that. Instead of guessing what you need upfront, we start by aligning the entire team—developers, the mission sponsor, contracting, security—on the mission priority. Then, we build in two-week cycles. Every two weeks, operators see new capability and give feedback. That feedback goes straight into the next cycle. This means we are not surprised at the end; we are aligned every two weeks. We deliver Initial Operational Capability in weeks, not months. And by the time we hit IOC, everyone—the sponsor, the operators, the security team—has been part of the design. No surprises. No big rework at the end. Lower risk. Better results. That is REDM."

This pitch works because it is jargon-free and outcome-focused. It does not talk about 'sprints' or 'ceremonies' or 'backlogs.' It talks about the problem (slow, surprising delivery) and the solution (fast, aligned delivery). It also gives the listener concrete imagery: two-week cycles, operators using the system, feedback flowing back into the next cycle. After this pitch, the listener should understand what REDM is and why they should care. Everything else is detail.

What REDM Replaces vs. What It Keeps

Use this table to clarify what changes and what stays the same. Often, stakeholders worry that REDM will throw out proven practices. This table reassures them that governance, discipline, and accountability all remain—only the timeline and the learning loop are different.

|  |  |
| Traditional Approach | REDM Approach |
| Ceremonies are meetings about requirements, design, and implementation. The product owner attends every meeting but rarely makes decisions in real time. | Engagement is co-design work. The product owner and the development team build together, making decisions about priority, trade-offs, and risk as the system takes shape. |
| Static requirements. Locked at the beginning. Changes go through a change control board and often get deferred to Phase 2. | Continuous feedback. Requirements emerge through the first two weeks of building. Stakeholders see what is possible, what is hard, and what creates the most mission value. |

Handling Common Objections

You will hear the same objections over and over. Here is how to respond to each one. The key is to not get defensive. Every objection is a real concern. Address it directly.

'Isn't this just Agile with a new name?'

Fair question. Agile is a 25-year-old movement, and most people have heard of it. So why a new name? Because REDM is Agile applied to a specific context: rapid capability delivery inside government. REDM also makes some specific choices that pure Agile does not mandate. For example, REDM starts with a Mission Alignment phase where the entire team—the sponsor, the developers, security, compliance—sits down and builds a shared understanding of the mission priority, the constraints, and the risks. This is not a requirements-gathering session; it is a design session. By the time that phase is done, everyone knows what success looks like and why the order of features matters. Pure Agile does not require this. REDM also commits to fixed timelines for IOC, which gives the sponsor confidence. REDM is Agile, but with government-specific structure built in. The answer to the question is: 'It is Agile applied to government delivery, with the mission priority and governance built in from the start. Agile is the approach; REDM is the application.'

'How do we know it will stay on budget and schedule?'

This is the PM's question, and it is the most important one. The answer is: REDM commits to a fixed timeline and a fixed team cost, and lets the scope vary. In other words, you commit to paying for, say, four developers for twelve weeks. That is your budget. What gets built depends on what the product owner prioritizes, but the cost and the time are fixed. This is actually more conservative than traditional waterfall, which fixes both scope and time, leaving budget as the only variable. When requirements are wrong (which they are), waterfall teams either blow the budget or blow the schedule. REDM handles requirements being wrong by reprioritizing—which features are most important—not by running over time or budget. The timeline is not 'we will be done when we finish all the features'; it is 'we will deliver the most important capabilities by this date, and stakeholders will see progress every two weeks.' Additionally, REDM typically delivers working capability faster and for less money than waterfall because feedback comes sooner. Problems surface early, and rework cycles are smaller. By the time you have spent twelve weeks and the full budget, you have a working system that the sponsor has been part of designing. You are not sitting across from the sponsor, explaining that the system does not work as expected and you need another three months and a million dollars to fix it.

'Can it meet our compliance requirements?'

Absolutely. In fact, REDM handles compliance better than traditional approaches because compliance is not treated as a phase that happens after feature development. It is built into the sprint cadence. The product owner—who understands both the mission and the compliance constraints—prioritizes compliance work in every sprint. If the system needs to be IL5/IL6 (Impact Level 5 or 6, per NIST standards), or if it needs CAC/PIV authentication, these requirements go into the backlog and are treated like any other priority. They get done in the early sprints, not at the end. By the time you reach IOC, the system is already compliant. This actually reduces risk compared to waterfall, where compliance is bolted on at the end and often requires rework of the application architecture. The answer is: 'REDM treats governance and security as first-class priorities, not afterthoughts. They are part of every sprint. This actually accelerates compliance because we do not discover compliance violations at the end—we build compliantly from the start.'

'What if requirements change?'

They will. That is the whole point. Traditional delivery assumes requirements are stable and knowable upfront. They usually are not. REDM assumes requirements will change—because the sponsor learns more by seeing what is possible, because the mission evolves, because the threat environment shifts. REDM is designed for that. When requirements change in REDM, you do not renegotiate the timeline or the budget. You reprioritize. The product owner looks at the new requirement, evaluates it against existing priorities, and decides what moves down in the queue. Change is absorbed by the cadence, not treated as risk. In fact, the ability to change is a strength: if a new mission priority emerges mid-project, you can pivot into it without blowing the entire plan. The answer is: 'Change is not a risk in REDM; it is expected. Every sprint, we ask: what is the highest-priority thing we can deliver next? That might change because the mission changed, or because we learned something from the operators. That is how we stay aligned with the actual mission.'

'We need documentation.'

You will get it. But not the kind of documentation that people usually ask for. In traditional delivery, the team produces a 500-page requirements document at the beginning, a 300-page design document in the middle, and a 200-page test plan at the end. Most of these documents are wrong by the time anyone reads them. They are hard to maintain and easy to ignore. REDM produces a decision log—a structured record of every feature that was considered, why it was prioritized (or deprioritized), and what trade-offs were made. It also produces a working system with clear code patterns, documented dependencies, and a tested architecture that the next team can understand and modify. It produces a sustainment-ready codebase, not stale documents. It produces a deployment guide and a runbook for operators, because these are actually used. The answer is: 'REDM does not skip documentation. It focuses on documentation that people actually read and maintain: the code, the decision log, the runbook, and the architecture patterns. We do not produce documents that go stale because we are focused on keeping the system alive.'

What to Show, Not Just Tell

The most common mistake in pitching REDM is leading with slides. You describe the methodology, you draw process diagrams, you talk about two-week cycles and continuous feedback. Stakeholders nod politely and then ask for a proposal. What you should do instead is show them something that works.

A five-minute demo of a working Power Platform app is more persuasive than a 30-slide deck about REDM philosophy. Here is why: when stakeholders see a working app, even a simple prototype, they immediately think about using it. They ask questions: 'Can it do X?' 'How would we enter Y?' 'What about Z?' These are the questions that matter. They are the beginning of requirements, and they are grounded in something concrete. A slide deck about methodology is abstract. A working app is concrete. The demo does the work for you.

How to Structure the Demo Artifact

If you are pitching REDM and you want to bring a prototype, structure it around three things: the data model, the user workflow, and the security model.

First, show the data model. This is the set of tables or lists that hold the mission-critical information. For a hypothetical aviation maintenance system, the data model might be: aircraft, maintenance tasks, maintenance history, and spare parts inventory. Show how these are connected. Use real (or realistic) data. Let people see what a full record looks like. This grounds the conversation in the actual data they will be working with.

Second, show a user workflow. Pick the most common task that the system needs to support—in the maintenance example, it might be logging a maintenance action on an aircraft. Walk through the workflow in the prototype. Show the data entry screens, the validation rules, the confirmation. Show what the system does after the workflow completes (e.g., updates a status, sends an alert, logs the action). This is what people will use; make it real.

Third, show the security model. This is especially important in government. Do not skip it. Show how users authenticate (CAC, password, etc.), what data they can see (role-based access control), and what audit trails are captured. Demonstrating this early says that you take security seriously and that you are not building a toy. A quick demo of role-based filtering or an audit log goes a long way with security and compliance teams.

A prototype built around these three elements should take 3–5 days to build. It does not need to be production-ready. It does not need to be the final system. But it needs to be real enough that people can use it and immediately see the potential. That prototype becomes the artifact of the pitch. Instead of saying 'REDM means we will get to a working app fast,' you show them the app. They understand immediately.

Positioning Against Traditional Delivery

Use this comparison to help stakeholders understand what changes in a REDM engagement and what the benefits are. Frame it not as 'Agile is better than Waterfall' but as 'here is how REDM reduces risk and accelerates delivery.'

| Aspect | Traditional Delivery | REDM |
| Time to First Usable Capability | 3–6 months (or longer) | 2–6 weeks |
| Requirements Process | Locked at the beginning. Changes go through formal change control. | Defined at the start, refined continuously as stakeholders see the system. |
| User Engagement | Sprint reviews; stakeholders see work at the end of each sprint but do not participate in decisions. | Co-design; stakeholders and developers make priority and design decisions together, every sprint. |
| Governance and Security | Phase gates; security and compliance review happens after feature development. | Built-in; every sprint includes security, compliance, and governance work alongside features. |
| Typical Rework Rate | High (15–30%); large rework cycles at the end when requirements prove wrong. | Low (5–10%); small course corrections because feedback is continuous. |

Winning Language — What to Say and What to Avoid

Language matters. Stakeholders hear certain phrases and they immediately trust you, or they immediately doubt you. Here are the phrases that work for REDM, and the phrases to avoid.

|  |  |
| Say This | Avoid This |
| We commit to Initial Operational Capability in X weeks. | We will be done when requirements are finalized. |
| Governance and security are built from day one, not added later. | We will harden security after we deliver the first version. |
| Stakeholders co-design the system; you are part of every decision. | We will demo at the end of the sprint and get your feedback. |
| The product owner prioritizes continuously, based on mission value. | We manage the backlog and deliver based on story points. |
| We deliver a working system and a decision log; the code is the documentation. | We will produce a comprehensive requirements document and design specification. |
| Change is expected and managed by reprioritizing the backlog. | Change requests go through a formal change control process. |
| We reduce risk by getting feedback early and often. | We reduce risk by locking down requirements upfront. |
| Success is measured by mission capability deployed and being used. | Success is measured by on-time, on-budget delivery of planned scope. |

The Follow-Up — How to Turn Interest Into Action

You have pitched REDM. The stakeholder is interested. Now what? Do not send a proposal. Do not schedule a long requirements-gathering session. Do something better: propose a Phase 1 Mission Alignment engagement.

Phase 1: Mission Alignment (1–3 Days)

This is not a requirements-gathering session. It is a design session. Here is how to frame it: 'We propose a two-day working session with you, your operators, your security team, and our team. The goal is to align on the mission priority, the constraints, and the success criteria. We will walk out of those two days with a Mission Requirements Brief, a prioritized roadmap, and a clear definition of what Initial Operational Capability means for you. From there, you will know what a REDM engagement costs and what you will get for it.'

The deliverables from Phase 1 are: (1) a Mission Requirements Brief (2–5 pages), which articulates the mission outcome, the key success criteria, and the constraints (compliance, budget, timeline); (2) a prioritized feature roadmap (1–2 pages), which lists the capabilities in priority order, with rough estimates of effort for each; (3) a definition of IOC—the specific set of capabilities that constitute 'working and useful' for the first release; (4) a risk register, which identifies technical risks, compliance risks, and mitigation strategies; and (5) a rough cost and schedule estimate for Phase 2.

Phase 1 typically costs between $10,000 and $50,000 depending on complexity. It should be fixed-price. The sponsor sees, in two days, whether this team and this approach are the right fit. If yes, they move forward with Phase 2. If no, they have learned what they need to learn and can pursue another path. The point is: do not ask for a huge upfront commitment. Ask for a small, focused engagement that proves the value of the approach.

What to Offer as Next Steps

After Phase 1, the natural next step is Phase 2: Rapid Build. Here is how to position it: 'Based on the Phase 1 output, Phase 2 is a fixed-price engagement to deliver Initial Operational Capability by [date]. We will use the prioritized roadmap from Phase 1 to sequence the work. Every two weeks, you will see new capability and have an opportunity to adjust priorities if the mission changes. The timeline and the team are fixed; the scope is flexible.'

Phase 2 typically runs 4–12 weeks depending on complexity. A small system (50 records per entity, 3–5 user workflows) might take 4 weeks. A medium system (500 records per entity, 10–15 workflows) might take 8 weeks. A large system (5000+ records, 20+ workflows) might take 12 weeks. The contract should stipulate the Phase 2 scope based on the Phase 1 output, and then include options for Phase 3 (hardening and sustainment), Phase 4 (scaling to production), or Phase 5 (adding new capabilities based on early feedback).

| The fastest way to align stakeholders is to show them something that almost works. REDM is designed to get to almost working as fast as possible. |

This quote summarizes the entire REDM philosophy. Use it when you need to remind stakeholders why REDM works: because it is faster to iterate on something real than to argue about something abstract. The most expensive part of any project is the time spent getting alignment. REDM compresses that time by producing something real in weeks, not months. That something does not have to be perfect; it has to be good enough to generate conversation. Use this idea in your pitch whenever you need to justify why moving fast is not cutting corners—it is cutting the time spent on alignment, which is where most risk lives.
